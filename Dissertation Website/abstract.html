Abstract

Based on an analysis of recorded multiperson conversation, this dissertation examines the affect the linguistic variables of age and relationship have on the repairs used in two conversations - one among four young students and the other between three mature sisters. In particular, this dissertation focuses on three aspects of repair taken from previous research. These aspects are:


1)The preference system of self-repair and other-repair which states that self-repair is preferred and other-repair is dispreferred. It is found in the results that self-repair is not affected by linguistic variables, but other-repair can be affected, particularly by a strong, sibling relationship.

2)The unique ‘other-selection’ in other-initiated repair which comes from a variety of studies is used to clarify hearing, acceptability and understanding, used for affiliation, and used to have politeness words avoid face-threatening acts. The linguistic variables do affect each of these factors involved with this repair, however, it cannot be fully confirmed whether or not they do because of limited results.

3)The banter principle is used as mock politeness to show emotional closeness and intimacy between the speakers. The linguistic variables affect banter, but similarly to previous, there is not enough data to confirm whether or not this is true or just context-specific to the data of this dissertation.
